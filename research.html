Research

I am a self-described "computational seismologist who likes to go outside". While I spend most of my days at a computer, writing code, running simulations, and visualizing data, I also yearn to get my hands dirty, messing with electronics, deploying instruments, and connecting deeply with the places that I study. Each panel below describes a different aspect of my current or prospective research interests, click where available for more information.

Adjoint Tomography

My primary research interests lie in the field of seismic imaging (specifically, adjoint tomography), which means I use seismic waves and supercomputers to probe the structure of the Earth (think MRI or CT scans). I am primarily interested in imaging the Earth's crust, and how that relates to seismicity and tectonics of a region.

Open Source Software

To perform my research, I develop, maintain, and rely on open source software. I am a big proponent of openly available data and software to make science more transparent, accessible and reproducible.

Planetary Seismology

Seismology is the primary tool for understanding the interiors of other planets in our solar system. I am part of a NASA-funded project that aims to provide synthetic estimates of seismicity, wind noise, and ground motion for a potential future Venus lander to help constrain instrument design criteria (picture from NASA).

Nuclear Treaty Monitoring

Modern instrumental seismology was partially born out of a need to monitor for, and detect nuclear tests. This mission exists today through the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty (CTBT). Part of my research with the Wilson Alaska Technical Center focuses on improving monitoring capabilities for CTBT efforts.

Cryoseismology

The noisy, energetic nature of glaciers provides a great setting for applying seismology, which has the ability to tell us things about ice characteristics and dynamics, glacial hydrological systems, and ice-bed interactions. I am interested in glacier-based seismic field work in Alaska, and understanding what seismology can tell us about our icy friends.
